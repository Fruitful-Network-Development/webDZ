Deer Fencing

Deer fencing looks simple until you live with the problem.

In the Cuyahoga Valley, deer pressure can turn "we'll just grow more" into "we lost the bed." The solution is a decision tree: How large is the protected area? How persistent is deer traffic? Exclusion fence or partial deterrent? What can you build inside a national park?

Practical takeaway

  • Truly deer-proof fence is tall,often 8 feet or more. Cost can pencil out vs. repeated crop losses.
  • Inside the park, fencing touches cultural resources: archaeological review before putting posts in. Build lead time and consultation into planning.

In 2021 we erected a deer exclusion fence around our main 16-acre field,enabling fruit trees and protected vegetable ground. See Vegetables and Setting the Table for Fruit. Related: Transplants, CVNP. Sources: News & Sources.

Barn

A barn is not just storage,it's infrastructure, history, and a visual anchor of the valley.

In CVNP, barns sit at three intersections: working structures for daily farm use; historic cultural resources needing thoughtful treatment; and a landscape the park is preserving.

Planning a barn project

  • Treatment: preserve as-is, rehabilitate for ag use, or adapt for compatible new use. Secretary of the Interior's Standards and NPS Preservation Brief on barns guide this.
  • Scope: condition assessment (roof, framing, siding, foundation, drainage); intended use; work scope tied to preservation best practices; protecting farm function and historic character.

Stewardship in practice: compatible materials, documented changes, keeping a working building working. Related: CVNP, Deer Fencing, The Farm. News & Sources.

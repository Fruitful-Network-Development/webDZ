About Us

Trapp Family Farm is a family-run, mixed crop-and-livestock farm on Route 303 in Peninsula, part of the living agricultural landscape preserved inside Cuyahoga Valley National Park. Established in 2012 through the park's Countryside Initiative.

What we're about

  • Long-term leases with stewardship and public-facing education
  • Small-scale, hands-on daily decisions
  • Soil covered, animals rotated, land not overworked
  • Welcoming neighbors who want to learn where their food comes from

Who we are

Mark and Emily Trapp came to farming by bringing outside careers and experiences into a place where practical problem-solving matters. Mark has spoken publicly about moving from engineering toward farming after becoming more concerned with energy use and the modern food system. The farm's focus has stayed consistent: healthy soil, healthy plants, healthy animals, and a community that can support itself with local food.

Because the farm sits inside a national park, it's part of something bigger than one business.

You may encounter Trapp Family Farm at the on-site stand during growing season, through educational visits, and through the broader network of Countryside farms and markets that connect the park to regional eaters.

Trapp Family Farm sits along West Streetsboro Road in Peninsula,an easy-to-spot stretch of working fields inside Cuyahoga Valley National Park. This farm is part of the Countryside Initiative.

How we got here

The farm's current chapter began when the former Holland farmstead was restored and reoccupied by new farmers in 2012. Early program notes describe an abandoned, deteriorating property brought back to life through restoration and a competitive proposal process. What you see today,open fields, active barns, a functioning farmstead,reflects that partnership between public land stewardship and private farm work.

Horse-drawn cultivation in the valley.

Power on the farm

Trapp Family Farm uses draft horses rather than tractors for key fieldwork,for practicality, energy stewardship, and a pace that suits small-scale, diverse production. Horses do both "big" work (pulling tillage tools) and delicate work (cultivating between vegetable rows). This is a mixed farm: crops and livestock are managed together. Seasonal eggs, produce, and meat are sold on-site; during growing season, a farm stand at the entrance is stocked with whatever is at peak harvest.

Inspired by

Our farm draws its inspiration from many sources. It began in earnest with a trip to Burlington, Vt and upstate NY in November 2010,a mission to discover Essex Farm in Essex, NY and see a modern horse-powered farm. Benner and Dana at Intervale in Burlington welcomed me into their farm field and later home. They introduced me to the Small Farmer's Journal and Eric and Anne Nordell's work at Beech Grove Farm,their approach to "Weeding the Soil, Not the Crop" and their market garden and cover-cropping methodology. I still recall watching that presentation, in awe, 15 years ago as I crafted a plan for a future farm. It has not lost its effect.

Our draft horses are at the heart of how we work the land,reducing reliance on fossil fuels and keeping a pace that fits small-scale, diverse production. They move our flocks, till our fields, and connect us to a tradition of human and animal power that remains practical and meaningful today.

Countryside

"Countryside" is the reason farms like this still exist in the middle of a national park.

Cuyahoga Valley's Countryside Initiative was created to preserve the valley's rural heritage while protecting natural and cultural resources. The park restores and maintains historic farm properties, then leases them to farmers who agree to operate with sustainable land practices and a public-facing educational mindset.

How Countryside shows up for visitors

  • The farms: distinct farmsteads (vegetables, livestock, orchards, vineyards) under park lease.
  • Markets and outreach: year-round Saturday markets,outdoor in warm months, indoor in winter.
  • Farmer training: the New Farmer Academy places interns on farms with hands-on work and structured learning.

Trapp Family Farm participates as a working farm and, at times, as a mentor site. The benefit is mutual: the program keeps agricultural land in active stewardship, and farmers gain long-term access to land in a high-visibility place, paired with a built-in audience that wants to learn and buy locally.

If you're trying to understand "why a farm is here," Countryside is the answer,a practical solution that keeps farmland from becoming "museum landscape" or development by keeping fields productive, barns used, and rural skills visible.

Cuyahoga Valley National Park

Cuyahoga Valley National Park is more than trails and waterfalls,it is also a working landscape with farms, barns, and cultural resources across the valley. That mix is intentional.

Two things at once

  • These farms are real, producing food with real constraints and real schedules.
  • They are not visitor attractions first; access varies. Many farms are only open during events or stand hours.

Farming here works differently than on private land. The park's farm program invites farmers to lease park-owned properties but also sets expectations: sustainable management rules, resource protection, and the reality that cultural resources (archaeology, historic structures) can affect what you can build and when.

The year-round Saturday farmers market is usually the most consistent entry point. When you visit farm sites, assume unpaved surfaces, occasional mud, and limited accessibility compared with a visitor center. Visiting (or driving by) is a way to see what the park is protecting: not only "nature," but a living rural heritage.

**Abstract** 

Consumer interest in local, diverse, and healthier food is substantial, yet local agriculture remains economically disadvantaged relative to centralized supply chains. This report explains that contradiction through official USDA data and peer-reviewed research rather than personal or project-based sources. The evidence shows that local food operates inside a market where success depends not only on production, but also on logistics, processing, procurement, compliance, and administrative coordination. USDA statistics indicate that local food sales are meaningful but still small relative to total agricultural sales, and they increasingly depend on intermediary and institutional channels rather than farmers markets alone. USDA's Food Dollar framework further shows that only a small share of consumer food spending returns to farms, while most of the food dollar is absorbed by marketing services such as transportation, labor, processing, and retailing. Research on food hubs, retailer standards, and food safety compliance shows that aggregation can help smaller producers reach larger markets, but fixed costs, pricing pressure, seasonality, and buyer requirements continue to weigh more heavily on small and regional farms than on larger suppliers. Overall, the literature indicates that local agriculture is disadvantaged less by consumer indifference than by structural economic and logistical barriers. 

**Introduction** 

Local agriculture is often discussed as something consumers value. Many buyers associate local food with freshness, health, regional identity, and support for nearby communities. Yet those preferences do not automatically produce strong market outcomes. Small and regional producers still struggle to compete consistently on price, volume, reliability, and procurement access against larger, more centralized supply chains. The central question of this report is therefore not whether local food is valued in principle, but why that value so often fails to become durable economic advantage. 

This report answers that question by synthesizing official USDA publications, federal statistical reports, and peer-reviewed studies on local food marketing, food hubs, compliance costs, and consumer willingness to pay. Across these sources, the same pattern appears: local agriculture operates inside a food system that rewards low-friction procurement, scale 

economies, standardized output, and predictable delivery. The problem is not simply weak demand. It is that the economic infrastructure surrounding food sales favors suppliers that can reduce coordination burden across transportation, distribution, invoicing, food safety, and buyer administration (USDA ERS, 2010; USDA ERS, 2015). 

The report uses a primarily causal analytical approach. Its purpose is to explain the structural reasons local agriculture remains economically disadvantaged despite visible consumer interest. In keeping with that purpose, the report focuses on external evidence and avoids advancing a personal proposal or platform as the solution. The goal is to clarify the problem itself, show how the major causes connect, and identify the factors most consistently supported by the literature.  
**Market Context** 

Official data show that local food is economically meaningful, but still limited relative to the broader food economy. ERS reported that sales of local edible farm products reached $11.8 billion in 2017, or about 3 percent of all agricultural sales in that year. NASS later reported $9.0 billion in direct farm sales of local edible food commodities in 2020\. That same report showed that local food scaling no longer depends mainly on direct-to-consumer sales. Institutions and intermediaries accounted for 46 percent of direct local food sales in 2020, while direct-to-consumer channels accounted for 33 percent (Martinez, 2021; USDA NASS, 2022). 

These figures matter because they show two things at once. First, local food is not a fringe activity; it already supports substantial farm revenue. Second, local food remains structurally constrained because larger sales opportunities increasingly sit in intermediary and institutional channels that demand steady volume, standardization, scheduling, and administrative consistency. In other words, consumer interest alone is not enough. Expanding local agriculture requires access to the same kinds of channels that also impose the most demanding coordination and compliance expectations (USDA ERS, 2011; USDA ERS, 2015; USDA NASS, 2022).

**Where the Economic Disadvantage Comes From** 

One of the clearest explanations appears in USDA's Food Dollar framework. The Food Dollar Series shows that the consumer food dollar is dominated by marketing costs such as transportation, labor, processing, packaging, and retail or foodservice activity. Under the marketing-bill method, the farm share of the food dollar was 15.9 cents in 2023, meaning that competition in food markets is shaped heavily by what happens after production rather than by farm output alone (USDA ERS, 2026). 

That structure favors supply chains that already operate efficiently at scale across post-farm stages. Local producers, by contrast, often move smaller volumes over more fragmented routes and with fewer dedicated logistics assets. The resulting disadvantage is not merely that local food can cost more to grow. It is that local food must also absorb higher  
per-unit coordination, assembly, and distribution costs. Modeling of fresh produce aggregation hubs similarly finds that economies of scale strongly affect the number, location, and cost structure of viable hubs, reinforcing the importance of scale even in systems designed to support regional food distribution (Ge et al., 2019). 

The literature therefore points away from a simple producer-side explanation. Local farms are not disadvantaged only because they are smaller. They are disadvantaged because food markets reward systems that lower the administrative and logistical burden of purchasing, moving, and standardizing food. A farm may produce a desirable product, but if the surrounding system makes that product harder to source, compare, or deliver reliably, market preference will tend to favor a less locally responsive but more operationally convenient alternative. 

**Distribution, Procurement, and Coordination Barriers** 

The type of sales channel available to a farm strongly shapes whether local production can remain financially viable. Direct-to-consumer outlets such as farmers markets, farm stands, and community supported agriculture can help producers keep more value per sale, but these channels are labor-intensive and limited in volume. Intermediated channels such as restaurants, groceries, schools, hospitals, and distributors can move more product, but they demand consistency in quantity, timing, food safety documentation, and pricing. That creates a difficult position for many local farms: the channels that can absorb more volume are also the channels that are hardest to enter and maintain (USDA ERS, 2011; USDA ERS, 2015). 

Research on consumer barriers reinforces this point from the demand side. Studies of local-food buyers and nonbuyers show that price and availability remain recurring barriers for both groups. From the buyer's perspective, food is not evaluated only by its ethical or geographic value. It is also evaluated by how easy it is to source, compare, order, receive, and substitute. When procurement systems are built around predictable, standardized suppliers, locally variable production can appear less competitive even when the food itself is valued more highly by consumers (Qi et al., 2017). 

This is where coordination becomes central. Local producers often face fragmented ordering systems, uncertain demand, shorter shelf life, and greater exposure to mismatch between what is produced and what buyers are prepared to purchase. These conditions increase risk and transaction cost. The economic problem is therefore not only one of scale, but of coordination burden. Producers and buyers may both have an interest in local trade, yet still fail to transact efficiently because the infrastructure connecting them is weaker than that supporting large-scale suppliers. 

**Food Hubs and Regional Aggregation** 

Food hubs and other regional aggregation models are often presented as one of the main responses to these structural barriers. USDA's Regional Food Hub Resource Guide defines food hubs as businesses or organizations that actively manage the aggregation, distribution, and  
marketing of source-identified products from local and regional producers in order to reach larger markets. In practical terms, hubs reduce transaction costs by pooling product, organizing logistics, and creating repeatable buyer relationships that individual farms may not be able to support alone (USDA AMS, 2012). 

Even so, the evidence shows that aggregation is helpful but not effortless. Findings from the 2019 National Food Hub Survey reported that about two-thirds of surveyed hubs were breaking even or better, while also identifying persistent barriers such as meeting buyer pricing requirements, balancing supply and demand, handling seasonality, and maintaining sufficient volume. Case-study and regional-impact research similarly suggest that food hubs operate as hybrids: they can improve market access and regional circulation of value, but they still face the economic pressures of mainstream procurement systems (Michigan State University Center for Regional Food Systems & Wallace Center at Winrock International, 2020; Cleveland et al., 2014; Jablonski et al., 2016). 

The importance of food hubs in this report is analytical rather than promotional. Their existence helps demonstrate that local agriculture's disadvantage is not simply a matter of product quality or consumer sentiment. It is a matter of infrastructure. When local systems introduce aggregation, routing, shared handling, or repeatable market access, some of the disadvantage can be reduced. At the same time, the evidence makes clear that these mechanisms do not eliminate the pressures of scale, buyer standards, or price competition. 

**Compliance Costs and Buyer Standards** 

Food safety regulation and buyer standards add another layer of structural disadvantage for small and regional farms. USDA estimated that the cost of complying with the Food Safety Modernization Act's Produce Rule falls much more heavily on smaller farms when measured as a share of sales, making compliance behave like a fixed-cost barrier rather than a neutral administrative requirement. Retailer requirements can be stricter still. In a USDA case study of produce retailers, interviews showed that retailers were likely to continue requiring third-party audits even though those audits are not mandated by the Produce Rule itself (USDA ERS, 2018; Minor et al., 2019). 

Farm-level studies help show what these burdens look like in practice. Research on Vermont fresh produce farms found that GAP certification imposed both acreage-based costs and several additional hours of labor each week during the growing season. Other studies found that food safety investments can produce benefits, especially for farms with third-party audits or wholesale orientation, but the financial and organizational burden still rises with implementation. For larger farms these demands can be spread across more output. For smaller farms they can function as barriers to entry into the very channels that offer the best opportunity to scale (Becot et al., 2012; Schmit et al., 2020). 

This point matters because it complicates the claim that local food simply needs more demand. Even where demand exists, the route into larger retail and institutional markets is  
filtered through compliance and administrative requirements that are more difficult for smaller farms to absorb. The result is that local agriculture may be valued by consumers but still excluded from the organizational settings where sustained sales growth would otherwise occur. 

**Why Consumer Interest Alone Does Not Solve the Problem** 

A common argument is that local agriculture would become more viable if consumers simply cared enough or paid higher prices. The literature does not support such a simple explanation. Meta-regression research on willingness to pay for local food found substantial variation across products, methods, and settings, along with evidence of publication selection bias that tends to favor larger positive estimates. More recent field evidence is even more cautionary. A field experiment on local labeling found no positive effect from a generic 'locally produced' label on willingness to pay (Printezis et al., 2019; Davidson et al., 2024). 

These findings do not mean consumers reject local food. They mean that stated preference is often weaker than actual purchasing behavior once price, convenience, and availability enter the decision. Studies of buyer and nonbuyer behavior similarly found that price and availability remain important barriers even among consumers already inclined toward local food. As a result, demand-side support alone does not reliably generate the premium needed to overcome higher coordination, compliance, and distribution costs (Qi et al., 2017). 

The broader implication is that consumer interest and market advantage are not equivalent. Consumers may value local food, yet still purchase according to convenience, price, or store availability. Buyers in institutions and retail settings may express support for local sourcing, yet still default to suppliers that better satisfy procurement routines. The evidence therefore supports a structural conclusion: local food can be valued, yet still disadvantaged, when the surrounding market rewards standardized low-friction procurement more than geographic proximity or product diversity. 

**Conclusion** 

The research reviewed here supports a consistent conclusion. Local agriculture remains economically disadvantaged not because it lacks value, but because it operates inside a food system organized around scale, coordination efficiency, and procurement routines that favor concentrated suppliers. USDA data show that local food sales are real and significant, yet still small relative to total agricultural sales and increasingly dependent on intermediary and institutional channels. The Food Dollar framework shows why this matters: the decisive cost pressures are often in transportation, processing, labor, and selling rather than at the farm gate itself (Martinez, 2021; USDA NASS, 2022; USDA ERS, 2026). 

The strongest explanation is therefore structural. Local farms face higher per-unit coordination costs, more fragmented logistics, tighter administrative burdens, and compliance requirements that behave like fixed costs. Aggregation, food hubs, and shared services can reduce some of those burdens, but they do not eliminate the underlying pressures created by  
centralized procurement systems. Consumer interest remains relevant, but it is not sufficient on its own. The evidence indicates that any serious effort to improve the competitiveness of local agriculture must address coordination, distribution, compliance, and buyer-side procurement structures rather than relying on preference alone.
